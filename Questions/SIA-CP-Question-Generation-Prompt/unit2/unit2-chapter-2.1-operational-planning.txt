planning prevents poor performance. CPOs must understand how to plan 
operations systematically, write operational orders, conduct briefings, 
and develop contingency plans for when things go wrong.

TOPIC 2.1.1: THE PLANNING CYCLE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Why Planning Matters:
- Ensures mission success
- Reduces risk to principal
- Coordinates team efforts
- Prepares for contingencies
- Professional standard

The Planning Process:

1. RECEIVE THE TASK:
   - Client brief
   - Principal requirements
   - Dates and locations
   - Resources available
   - Budget constraints

2. GATHER INFORMATION:
   - Principal profile
   - Threat assessment
   - Venue information
   - Route options
   - Local intelligence

3. ANALYSE:
   - Risk assessment
   - Resource requirements
   - Timeline development
   - Options comparison
   - Constraints identified

4. DEVELOP PLAN:
   - Concept of operations
   - Detailed tasks
   - Timings
   - Communications
   - Contingencies

5. PRODUCE ORDERS:
   - Written operational order
   - Maps and diagrams
   - Contact details
   - Distribution

6. BRIEF THE TEAM:
   - Deliver briefing
   - Confirm understanding
   - Issue orders
   - Equipment check
   - Questions

7. EXECUTE:
   - Implement plan
   - Monitor progress
   - Adapt as needed
   - Communicate changes

8. REVIEW:
   - Debrief
   - Lessons learned
   - Documentation
   - Improvements identified

Time Available:
- Planning takes time
- More complex = more time
- Last minute = risk
- Ongoing operations = continuous planning

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 2.1.2: OPERATIONAL ORDERS (OP ORDERS)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Purpose of Op Orders:
- Document the plan
- Communicate to team
- Reference during operation
- Accountability
- Legal record

Standard Format - Five Paragraph Order:

1. SITUATION:
   - Background
   - Principal details
   - Threat assessment
   - Friendly forces (who's helping)
   - Attachments/detachments

2. MISSION:
   - Clear statement
   - What we will do
   - When and where
   - Purpose/effect

3. EXECUTION:
   - Concept of operations
   - Detailed tasks
   - Coordinating instructions
   - Timings
   - Actions on

4. SERVICE SUPPORT:
   - Equipment
   - Dress code
   - Transport
   - Medical
   - Meals/accommodation

5. COMMAND AND SIGNALS:
   - Chain of command
   - Communications plan
   - Radio frequencies
   - Call signs
   - Code words
   - Reporting

Writing Clear Orders:
- Simple language
- Unambiguous
- Task, conditions, standards
- Who, what, when, where, why
- Measurable outcomes

Distribution:
- Need to know basis
- Secure handling
- Confirmed receipt
- Updates distributed
- Version control

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 2.1.3: STANDARD OPERATING PROCEDURES (SOPs)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

What are SOPs:
- Pre-agreed procedures
- Routine situations
- No orders needed
- Team knows automatically
- Consistency

Purpose of SOPs:
- Speed of response
- Reduced communication
- Consistency
- Training standard
- Professional operations

Types of SOPs:

MOVEMENT SOPs:
- Formation drills
- Vehicle procedures
- Route changes
- Arrival/departure

EMERGENCY SOPs:
- Contact drills
- Evacuation
- Medical emergency
- Fire

COMMUNICATION SOPs:
- Radio procedures
- Reporting format
- Check times
- Code words

ADMINISTRATIVE SOPs:
- Shift changes
- Equipment checks
- Documentation
- Handover

SOP vs Op Order:
- SOP = how we always do it
- Op Order = specific to this task
- SOPs reduce Op Order length
- Reference SOPs in orders
- "IAW SOP" (In Accordance With)

Developing SOPs:
- Based on best practice
- Team input
- Tested and refined
- Documented
- Trained regularly

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 2.1.4: EMERGENCY OPERATING PROCEDURES (EOPs)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

What are EOPs:
- Response to emergencies
- Pre-planned actions
- Immediate implementation
- Life-threatening situations

Difference from SOPs:
- SOP = routine operations
- EOP = emergency response
- EOP activated by trigger
- Often time-critical
- Higher stress

Types of EOPs:

ATTACK EOP:
- Armed attack
- Unarmed attack
- Vehicle attack
- IED/bomb

MEDICAL EOP:
- Principal medical emergency
- Team member injury
- Bystander emergency
- Evacuation to hospital

FIRE/EVACUATION EOP:
- Building fire
- Emergency evacuation
- Assembly points
- Principal safety

VEHICLE EOP:
- Accident
- Ambush
- Vehicle failure
- Route blocked

COMMUNICATION EOP:
- Radio failure
- Lost contact
- Emergency signals
- Rally points

EOP Components:
- Trigger (what activates it)
- Immediate actions
- Roles and responsibilities
- Communication
- Recovery/continuation

Transitioning SOP to EOP:
- Recognition of trigger
- Clear communication
- Immediate action
- Team coordination
- Principal safety priority

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 2.1.5: CONTINGENCY PLANNING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

What is Contingency Planning:
- Planning for "what if"
- Alternative actions
- When plan fails
- Unexpected events

Why Contingencies Matter:
- No plan survives contact
- Things go wrong
- Adaptability required
- Reduces reaction time
- Professional standard

Types of Contingencies:

ROUTE CONTINGENCIES:
- Primary route blocked
- Traffic problems
- Accident ahead
- Alternate routes

VENUE CONTINGENCIES:
- Venue compromised
- Change of location
- Security breach
- Weather impact

PERSONNEL CONTINGENCIES:
- Team member illness
- Injury during operation
- Principal unavailable
- Resource shortage

THREAT CONTINGENCIES:
- Elevated threat
- Specific intelligence
- Surveillance detected
- Attack imminent

MEDICAL CONTINGENCIES:
- Hospital locations
- Medical facilities
- Doctor on call
- Medication needs

"Actions On" Planning:
- Actions on attack
- Actions on accident
- Actions on medical emergency
- Actions on fire
- Actions on lost comms

Contingency Documentation:
- Part of Op Order
- Briefed to team
- Locations known
- Contact numbers ready
- Equipment prepared

TOPIC 2.1.6: BRIEFINGS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Purpose of Briefings:
- Communicate plan
- Ensure understanding
- Coordinate team
- Confirm readiness
- Opportunity for questions

Types of Briefings:

INITIAL BRIEF:
- Full operation overview
- Complete Op Order
- All team members
- Detailed information

CONFIRMATORY BRIEF:
- Before execution
- Confirm key points
- Any changes
- Final questions
- Equipment check

UPDATE BRIEF:
- During operation
- New information
- Plan changes
- Continuing guidance

SPECIALIST BRIEF:
- Specific roles
- Technical information
- Individual tasks
- Coordination points

Briefing Structure:

OPENING:
- Get attention
- State purpose
- Outline structure

BODY:
- Situation
- Mission
- Execution
- Support
- Communications

CLOSING:
- Summary key points
- Confirm understanding
- Questions
- Time check
- Synchronise watches

Briefing Techniques:
- Clear and confident
- Logical sequence
- Visual aids (maps, diagrams)
- Check understanding
- Allow questions
- Written backup

Common Briefing Errors:
- Too much detail
- Unclear priorities
- No questions allowed
- Running over time
- Assuming understanding

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 2.1.7: DEBRIEFINGS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Purpose of Debriefings:
- Review operation
- Identify lessons
- Improve future ops
- Team feedback
- Documentation

When to Debrief:
- After every operation
- After significant incidents
- At shift change
- Periodic reviews
- Training exercises

Debrief Structure:

1. WHAT WAS PLANNED:
   - Original objectives
   - Tasks assigned
   - Expected outcomes

2. WHAT HAPPENED:
   - Actual events
   - Sequence of actions
   - Deviations from plan

3. WHY:
   - Reasons for success
   - Causes of problems
   - External factors

4. LESSONS LEARNED:
   - What worked well
   - What needs improvement
   - Recommendations

5. ACTIONS:
   - Changes to implement
   - Training needs
   - SOP updates
   - Follow-up required

Debrief Principles:
- No blame culture
- Honest assessment
- Everyone contributes
- Focus on improvement
- Document outcomes

Hot Debrief:
- Immediately after
- Quick key points
- While fresh
- Brief format

Cold Debrief:
- Later, more formal
- Full analysis
- Written record
- More objective

MUST GET CORRECT:

PLANNING CYCLE (8 stages):

FIVE PARAGRAPH ORDER:

SOP vs EOP:

- EOP = Emergency Operating Procedures (emergencies)
