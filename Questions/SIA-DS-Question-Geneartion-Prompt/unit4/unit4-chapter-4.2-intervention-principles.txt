TOPIC 4.2.1: STANCE AND POSITIONING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The Interview Stance:
- Non-aggressive but ready
- One foot slightly behind the other
- Weight evenly distributed
- Knees slightly bent
- Hands visible (at chest level)
- Balanced and mobile
- Angled 45 degrees to person

Why 45-Degree Angle:
- Less confrontational than square-on
- Presents smaller target
- Better balance
- Easier to move
- Can still see them clearly

Reactionary Gap:
- Distance that allows reaction time
- Approximately arm's length plus
- Increases with threat level
- Allows time to defend or escape
- Adjust based on assessment

Weight Distribution:
- Balanced stance
- Able to move in any direction
- Not overcommitted
- Ready to advance or retreat
- Low centre of gravity

Hand Position:
- Visible and open
- At chest height
- Palms out or hands together
- Ready but not threatening
- Can raise quickly to defend

Common Mistakes:
- Hands in pockets
- Crossed arms
- Too close
- Too square-on
- Weight on one foot
- Leaning back

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 4.2.2: TACTICAL POSITIONING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Relative Positioning:

ANGLE:
- Never directly in front (target)
- Slightly to the side
- Off their centre line
- Harder for them to attack

DISTANCE:
- Close enough to intervene
- Far enough to react
- Adjust based on threat
- Closer for control, further for safety

HEIGHT:
- Stay on feet if possible
- Ground is disadvantageous
- Low centre of gravity helps
- Don't tower intimidatingly

Environmental Awareness:
- Know your surroundings
- Identify hazards
- Know escape routes
- Use environment tactically

Don't Get Cornered:
- Always have exit route
- Don't back into corners
- Don't let them get behind you
- Maintain options

Don't Corner Them:
- Leave them line of retreat
- Cornered person may fight
- Allows face-saving exit
- Unless containment required

Multiple Persons:
- Don't be surrounded
- Keep all in view
- Position to see all threats
- Back to wall if necessary

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 4.2.3: COMMUNICATION DURING PHYSICAL INTERVENTION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Verbal Commands:
- Short, clear commands
- Loud enough to hear
- Simple language
- Repeat if necessary

Command Structure:
- One word commands: "STOP!", "DOWN!", "MOVE!"
- Tell them what TO do (not just what not to do)
- Firm but controlled
- Not screaming

Why Verbal Commands Matter:
- May gain compliance (avoid force)
- Demonstrates you gave them chance
- Witnesses hear you trying to communicate
- Creates evidence of your intent

Continuous Communication:
- Explain what you're doing
- "I'm going to take your arm"
- "Walk with me to the exit"
- Reduces resistance
- Professional conduct

Warning Before Force:
- "If you don't stop, I will have to restrain you"
- Clear consequence
- Opportunity to comply
- Shows force was last resort

During Restraint:
- Reassurance
- Explain what's happening
- "If you calm down, I can release you"
- "I don't want to hurt you"
- Pathway to de-escalation

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 4.2.4: TEAMWORK AND COORDINATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Why Teamwork Essential:
- Safer for everyone
- More effective control
- Witnesses to each other's actions
- Can cover different angles
- Backup if needed

Roles in Team Intervention:

PRIMARY CONTROLLER:
- Takes lead on restraint
- Makes decisions
- Issues commands
- Communicates with subject

SECONDARY CONTROLLER:
- Supports primary
- Controls limbs
- Prevents escape
- Watches for weapons

OBSERVER/COMMUNICATOR:
- Monitors subject's condition
- Communicates with others (radio)
- Manages crowd
- Documents events
- Calls for help if needed

Coordination Principles:
- Clear who is in charge
- Pre-arranged signals
- Known roles
- Training together
- Brief before events if possible

Communication Between Team:
- Clear verbal signals
- Hand signals if noisy
- Confirm understanding
- Update on changes
- Call for more help early

Triangle Formation:
- Three people around aggressive person
- Each covers different angle
- Hard to escape all three
- Safer than one-on-one

When to Get Backup:
- Before you need it
- If outnumbered
- If weapons suspected
- If situation escalating
- If special risks (intoxication, size)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 4.2.5: NON-PAIN COMPLIANCE APPROACH
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

What is Non-Pain Compliance:
- Control without deliberately causing pain
- Uses balance, leverage, positioning
- More appropriate for security
- Less risk of injury
- More defensible

Why Avoid Pain Compliance:
- May escalate situation
- More risk of injury
- Harder to justify
- May cause fight response
- Professional standards issue

Principles of Control Without Pain:
- Control balance (off-balance person easier to control)
- Use body weight and leverage
- Mechanical advantage
- Guiding not forcing
- Work with their movement

Exceptions:
- Pain may be necessary for safety
- If non-pain methods failing
- Proportionate to threat
- Still minimum necessary

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 4.2.6: BALANCE AND LEVERAGE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Balance Principles:
- Person off-balance is easier to control
- Create instability
- Use their movement
- Lower your centre of gravity

Centre of Gravity:
- Located around belly button
- Control this to control person
- Lower is more stable
- Push/pull affects balance

Leverage:
- Use mechanical advantage
- Arms as levers
- Joints as fulcrums
- Less strength needed
- More control achieved

Applying Leverage:
- Straight arm vs bent arm
- Control at joints
- Wrist, elbow, shoulder
- Guide direction of travel

Body Weight:
- Use your weight, not just strength
- Drop weight into holds
- Body positioning matters
- Larger person can be controlled by smaller

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 4.2.7: THE FORCE CONTINUUM
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Graduated Response Model:

LEVEL 1 - PRESENCE:
- Your presence alone
- Uniform, posture, position
- May deter aggression
- No physical contact

LEVEL 2 - VERBAL:
- Communication
- Commands
- Persuasion
- Warning of consequences

LEVEL 3 - SOFT CONTROL:
- Touch/guiding
- Escorting
- Open hand techniques
- Minimal force

LEVEL 4 - HARD CONTROL:
- Restraint holds
- Take-downs
- Joint locks (if trained)
- More forceful control

LEVEL 5 - DEFENSIVE STRIKES:
- Strikes to disengage
- Creating distance
- Last resort before deadly force
- Still proportionate

The continuum is not strictly linear:
- Can enter at any level based on threat
- Must match response to threat
- Can escalate or de-escalate
- Constant reassessment

Matching Response to Threat:
- Minor threat = lower level response
- Serious threat = higher level may be immediate
- Always proportionate
- Always minimum necessary

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 4.2.8: DECISION MAKING UNDER PRESSURE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Stress Effects on Decision Making:
- Tunnel vision
- Auditory exclusion
- Time distortion
- Fine motor skill loss
- Complex thought impaired

Overcoming Stress Effects:

TRAINING:
- Practice builds automatic responses
- Reduces need for complex thought
- Muscle memory
- Confidence from competence

BREATHING:
- Tactical breathing
- Slows heart rate
- Improves thinking
- 4 counts in, hold, out

SIMPLE PLANS:
- Pre-decided responses
- If X, then Y
- Reduces decision load
- Faster reaction

OODA Loop:
- OBSERVE: What's happening
- ORIENT: What does it mean
- DECIDE: What to do
- ACT: Do it
- Repeat continuously

Avoiding "Freezing":
- Training and practice
- Recognition of freeze response
- Pre-planned responses
- Trust your training

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 4.2.9: TIMING AND INITIATIVE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

When to Act:
- When threat is clear
- When attack is imminent
- When waiting increases danger
- When you have advantage

Seizing Initiative:
- Acting rather than reacting
- Controlling the situation
- Not waiting for worst case
- Proactive not passive

Acting Too Early:
- May not be justified
- May escalate situation
- May have been unnecessary
- Ensure legal justification

Acting Too Late:
- Gives them advantage
- May result in injury
- Harder to control
- Lost opportunity

Timing Considerations:
- Threat assessment
- Legal justification present
- Your readiness
- Backup available
- Environment factors

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 4.2.10: EXIT STRATEGIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Always Plan Exit:
- How will this end?
- What's the objective?
- How do you disengage?
- What if it goes wrong?

Possible Endings:
- Verbal compliance achieved
- Physical ejection completed
- Police arrival and handover
- Person calms and is released
- Tactical withdrawal
- Medical emergency response

Disengagement Points:
- When objective achieved
- When threat neutralised
- When safer option available
- When situation too dangerous

Failed Intervention:
- Know when to disengage
- Tactical retreat is valid
- Get help
- Don't fight to the death over entry fee

Post-Intervention Plan:
- Documentation
- Reporting
- Evidence preservation
- Welfare checks
- Debrief
