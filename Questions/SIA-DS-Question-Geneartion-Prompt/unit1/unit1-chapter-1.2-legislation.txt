TOPIC 1.2.1: CRIMINAL LAW VS CIVIL LAW
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Criminal Law:
- Offences against the state/society
- Prosecution by Crown Prosecution Service (CPS)
- Cases heard in Magistrates' Court or Crown Court
- Burden of proof: "beyond reasonable doubt"
- Penalties: imprisonment, fines, community orders
- Examples: assault, theft, criminal damage

Civil Law:
- Disputes between individuals or organisations
- Claimant brings case against defendant
- Cases heard in County Court or High Court
- Burden of proof: "balance of probabilities"
- Remedies: damages (compensation), injunctions
- Examples: negligence, breach of contract, trespass

Key Distinction:
- Same incident can be BOTH criminal and civil
- Example: Assault is criminal offence AND can lead to civil claim for damages
- Different courts, different standards of proof, different outcomes
- Acquittal in criminal court doesn't prevent civil claim

Security Operative Implications:
- Can face criminal prosecution for unlawful actions
- Can also be sued civilly for same actions
- Employer may be vicariously liable in civil cases

TOPIC 1.2.2: HUMAN RIGHTS ACT 1998
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Overview:
- Incorporates European Convention on Human Rights into UK law
- Public authorities must act compatibly with Convention rights
- Security operatives may be acting as agents of public authority
- Rights must be respected in all actions

Key Articles:

ARTICLE 2 - RIGHT TO LIFE:
- Duty to protect life
- Cannot take life except in extreme self-defence
- Duty to prevent foreseeable harm
- Absolute right (cannot be limited)

ARTICLE 3 - PROHIBITION OF TORTURE:
- No torture, inhuman or degrading treatment
- Covers physical and psychological abuse
- Absolute right (never justified)
- Relevant to restraint and treatment of detainees

ARTICLE 5 - RIGHT TO LIBERTY AND SECURITY:
- Cannot be deprived of liberty except lawfully
- Arrest must be lawful
- False imprisonment breaches this article
- Limited right (can be restricted in certain circumstances)

ARTICLE 6 - RIGHT TO FAIR TRIAL:
- Innocent until proven guilty
- Cannot prejudge or punish without trial
- Relevant when gathering evidence

ARTICLE 8 - RIGHT TO RESPECT FOR PRIVATE LIFE:
- Privacy of person, home, correspondence
- Includes surveillance, searches, CCTV
- Qualified right (can be balanced against other interests)
- Security must be proportionate

ARTICLE 10 - FREEDOM OF EXPRESSION:
- Right to hold and express opinions
- Can be restricted to prevent crime, protect others
- Qualified right

ARTICLE 11 - FREEDOM OF ASSEMBLY:
- Right to peaceful assembly
- Protests, gatherings
- Can be restricted for public safety
- Qualified right

ARTICLE 14 - PROHIBITION OF DISCRIMINATION:
- Rights apply equally to all
- Cannot discriminate in application of rights
- Works with other articles

Types of Rights:
- ABSOLUTE: Cannot be limited (Art 2, 3)
- LIMITED: Can only be limited in specific circumstances (Art 5)
- QUALIFIED: Can be balanced against other interests (Art 8, 10, 11)

Proportionality:
- Any interference with rights must be proportionate
- Least restrictive means to achieve legitimate aim
- Balance between individual rights and public interest

TOPIC 1.2.3: EQUALITY ACT 2010
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Overview:
- Consolidates and replaces previous discrimination legislation
- Protects people from discrimination in the workplace and wider society
- Applies to security operatives in their treatment of public

The 9 Protected Characteristics:
1. Age
2. Disability
3. Gender reassignment
4. Marriage and civil partnership
5. Pregnancy and maternity
6. Race (includes colour, nationality, ethnic origin)
7. Religion or belief (includes lack of belief)
8. Sex
9. Sexual orientation

NOT Protected (Common Misconceptions):
- Political opinion
- Social class
- Criminal record
- Appearance (unless related to protected characteristic)

Types of Discrimination:

DIRECT DISCRIMINATION:
- Treating someone worse BECAUSE OF protected characteristic
- Example: refusing entry because of race
- No justification possible (except age in limited circumstances)

INDIRECT DISCRIMINATION:
- Policy that applies to everyone but disadvantages a particular group
- Example: dress code that disadvantages religious groups
- Can be justified if "proportionate means of achieving legitimate aim"

HARASSMENT:
- Unwanted conduct related to protected characteristic
- Violates dignity or creates intimidating environment
- Includes sexual harassment
- Includes harassment by third parties

VICTIMISATION:
- Treating someone badly because they made a discrimination complaint
- Or because they supported someone else's complaint
- Protection for whistleblowers

Reasonable Adjustments:
- Duty to make adjustments for disabled persons
- Remove barriers that disadvantage disabled people
- May include: ramps, alternative formats, extra time, assistance

Application to Door Supervision:
- Admission policies must not discriminate
- Dress codes must be carefully considered
- Searches must be conducted fairly
- Refusing entry must be for legitimate, non-discriminatory reasons
- Accommodate disabled customers where reasonable

TOPIC 1.2.4: DATA PROTECTION ACT 2018 / UK GDPR
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Overview:
- Controls how personal information is used
- Applies to all organisations handling personal data
- Security operatives handle personal data regularly
- Serious penalties for breaches

The 7 Data Protection Principles:

1. LAWFULNESS, FAIRNESS, TRANSPARENCY:
   - Must have lawful basis for processing
   - Must be fair to the individual
   - Must be transparent about how data is used

2. PURPOSE LIMITATION:
   - Collect for specified, explicit, legitimate purposes
   - Don't use for incompatible purposes

3. DATA MINIMISATION:
   - Only collect what is necessary
   - Don't collect excessive data

4. ACCURACY:
   - Keep data accurate and up to date
   - Correct inaccurate data

5. STORAGE LIMITATION:
   - Don't keep data longer than necessary
   - Delete when no longer needed

6. INTEGRITY AND CONFIDENTIALITY (Security):
   - Keep data secure
   - Protect against unauthorised access
   - Protect against loss or damage

7. ACCOUNTABILITY:
   - Controller responsible for compliance
   - Must be able to demonstrate compliance

Lawful Bases for Processing:
- Consent
- Contract
- Legal obligation
- Vital interests
- Public task
- Legitimate interests

Special Category Data:
- More sensitive data requiring extra protection
- Includes: racial/ethnic origin, political opinions, religious beliefs,
  trade union membership, genetic data, biometric data, health data,
  sex life or sexual orientation, criminal convictions

Data Subject Rights:
- Right of access (subject access request)
- Right to rectification
- Right to erasure ("right to be forgotten")
- Right to restrict processing
- Right to data portability
- Right to object

Data Breach Reporting:
- Report to ICO (Information Commissioner's Office) within 72 HOURS
- If breach likely to result in risk to individuals
- Must also notify affected individuals in serious cases

Security Operative Duties:
- Keep personal information confidential
- Only access information on need-to-know basis
- Secure storage of records and notebooks
- Don't share information inappropriately
- CCTV footage is personal data - handle appropriately

CCTV Considerations:
- Signage required (people must know they're being recorded)
- Footage is personal data
- Access must be controlled
- Retention periods apply
- Subject access requests can include CCTV

TOPIC 1.2.5: HEALTH AND SAFETY LEGISLATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Health and Safety at Work Act 1974 (HASAWA):

SECTION 2 - Employer's Duty to Employees:
- Provide safe workplace
- Safe systems of work
- Safe equipment
- Adequate training
- Welfare provisions
- "So far as is reasonably practicable"

SECTION 3 - Employer's Duty to Non-Employees:
- Must not endanger visitors, customers, public
- Applies to anyone affected by the business

SECTION 7 - Employee's Duties:
- Take reasonable care for own health and safety
- Take reasonable care for others affected by your actions
- Cooperate with employer on health and safety matters

SECTION 8 - General Duty:
- No one shall intentionally or recklessly interfere with
  anything provided for health and safety
- Don't misuse safety equipment

"Reasonably Practicable":
- Weighing risk against cost/difficulty of reducing it
- Not absolute duty - what is reasonable
- Higher risk = more required to control it

Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999:
- Requires risk assessments
- Competent persons for health and safety
- Emergency procedures
- Information and training

Personal Protective Equipment (PPE):
- Employer must provide suitable PPE
- Employee must use PPE correctly
- Last resort in control hierarchy
- Types: high-vis, gloves, protective footwear

TOPIC 1.2.6: RIDDOR - REPORTING REQUIREMENTS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

RIDDOR = Reporting of Injuries, Diseases and Dangerous Occurrences 
Regulations 2013

What Must Be Reported:

DEATHS:
- Any work-related death
- Report immediately, then written report within 10 days

SPECIFIED INJURIES:
- Fractures (except fingers, thumbs, toes)
- Amputations
- Serious burns
- Loss of sight
- Crush injuries
- Injuries requiring immediate hospitalisation over 24 hours
- Report immediately, then written report within 10 days

OVER-7-DAY INJURIES:
- Injuries causing incapacitation for more than 7 consecutive days
- Report within 15 days

NON-WORKER INJURIES:
- Injuries to public requiring hospital treatment
- Report within 10 days

OCCUPATIONAL DISEASES:
- When diagnosed and linked to work
- Report when diagnosed

DANGEROUS OCCURRENCES:
- Near-misses with serious potential
- Report within 10 days

How to Report:
- Online at HSE website
- Telephone for fatalities and specified injuries
- Employer's duty to report (not employee)

Record Keeping:
- Keep records for at least 3 years
- Records of all reportable incidents
- Available for inspection

TOPIC 1.2.7: OTHER RELEVANT LEGISLATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Criminal Damage Act 1971:
- Intentionally or recklessly destroying/damaging property
- Without lawful excuse
- Offence even if own property (if endangers life)

Theft Act 1968:
- Dishonestly appropriating property
- Belonging to another
- With intention to permanently deprive

Public Order Act 1986:
- Various public order offences
- Section 4: fear or provocation of violence
- Section 5: harassment, alarm, distress
- Riot, violent disorder, affray

Protection from Harassment Act 1997:
- Course of conduct amounting to harassment
- Puts another in fear of violence
- Stalking offences
