TOPIC 1.2.1: HUMAN RIGHTS ACT 1998
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

What is the Human Rights Act:
- Incorporates European Convention on Human Rights into UK law
- Came into force 2000
- Protects fundamental rights and freedoms
- Public authorities must respect rights
- Individuals can challenge breaches in UK courts

Key Articles for CCTV Operators:

ARTICLE 2 - RIGHT TO LIFE:
- Duty to protect life
- CCTV can help protect life
- Monitoring vulnerable persons
- Directing emergency response
- Evidence for serious crimes

ARTICLE 3 - PROHIBITION OF TORTURE:
- No torture or inhumane treatment
- Relevant to dignity of those monitored
- Treatment of suspects
- How footage is used

ARTICLE 5 - RIGHT TO LIBERTY:
- No arbitrary detention
- CCTV evidence must be accurate
- Can affect someone's liberty
- Importance of correct identification

ARTICLE 6 - RIGHT TO FAIR TRIAL:
- CCTV as evidence must be reliable
- Integrity of footage
- Accurate time stamps
- Chain of custody

ARTICLE 8 - RIGHT TO RESPECT FOR PRIVATE LIFE:
- Most relevant for CCTV operators
- Privacy must be balanced with security
- Surveillance must be proportionate
- Legitimate aim required
- Not absolute right - can be limited

ARTICLE 10 - FREEDOM OF EXPRESSION:
- Right to receive and impart information
- CCTV in public protests
- Balance with security needs

ARTICLE 14 - PROHIBITION OF DISCRIMINATION:
- Rights apply to everyone equally
- No discrimination in application
- Monitoring must be fair

Balancing Rights:
- Rights are not absolute (except Article 3)
- Can be limited for legitimate purposes
- National security
- Public safety
- Prevention of crime
- Must be proportionate

Proportionality for CCTV:
- Is surveillance necessary?
- Is it the least intrusive option?
- Does it achieve legitimate aim?
- Are safeguards in place?

TOPIC 1.2.2: EQUALITY ACT 2010
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Purpose of the Act:
- Consolidates discrimination law
- Protects against discrimination
- Promotes equality of opportunity
- Applies to employment and services

The 9 Protected Characteristics:
1. Age
2. Disability
3. Gender reassignment
4. Marriage and civil partnership
5. Pregnancy and maternity
6. Race (including nationality, ethnicity)
7. Religion or belief
8. Sex
9. Sexual orientation

Types of Discrimination:

DIRECT DISCRIMINATION:
- Treating someone less favourably
- Because of protected characteristic
- Example: Monitoring only people of certain race

INDIRECT DISCRIMINATION:
- Policy applies to everyone
- But disadvantages particular group
- Cannot be justified
- Example: CCTV coverage ignoring disabled access areas

HARASSMENT:
- Unwanted conduct
- Related to protected characteristic
- Violates dignity or creates hostile environment
- Example: Making comments about monitored persons

VICTIMISATION:
- Treating someone badly
- Because they made complaint about discrimination
- Or supported someone who did

Relevance to CCTV Operators:
- Must not discriminate in monitoring
- Fair treatment of all persons
- No profiling based on protected characteristics
- Equal attention to all areas/people
- Treating colleagues fairly

Reasonable Adjustments:
- For people with disabilities
- Remove barriers
- Provide alternatives
- In control room: accessible workstations

TOPIC 1.2.3: DATA PROTECTION BASICS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Note: Detailed CCTV data protection is in Unit 2. This covers basics only.

Data Protection Act 2018 / UK GDPR:
- CCTV footage is personal data
- Images that identify individuals
- Regulated by Information Commissioner's Office (ICO)
- Heavy penalties for breaches

Key Principles (overview):
- Lawfulness, fairness, transparency
- Purpose limitation
- Data minimisation
- Accuracy
- Storage limitation
- Security
- Accountability

For CCTV Operators:
- Footage contains personal data
- Must be handled properly
- Access restricted
- Secure storage
- Appropriate retention
- Lawful disclosure only

Confidentiality:
- Don't discuss footage with unauthorised persons
- Don't share images inappropriately
- Don't access footage without reason
- Professional duty

Rights of Data Subjects:
- Right to be informed (signage)
- Right of access (Subject Access Request)
- Right to erasure (limited for CCTV)
- Right to object

TOPIC 1.2.4: HEALTH AND SAFETY AT WORK ACT 1974
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The Act:
- Primary health and safety legislation
- Duties on employers and employees
- Applies to all workplaces
- Enforced by HSE (Health and Safety Executive)

Employer Duties:
- Provide safe workplace
- Safe systems of work
- Information, instruction, training
- Personal protective equipment where needed
- Risk assessments
- Health and safety policy (5+ employees)

Employee Duties:
- Take reasonable care of self and others
- Cooperate with employer
- Don't interfere with safety equipment
- Report hazards
- Follow training and procedures

Control Room Health and Safety:
- Ergonomic workstations
- Display screen regulations
- Appropriate lighting
- Regular breaks
- Temperature control
- Lone working procedures

Display Screen Equipment Regulations 1992:
- Applies to CCTV operators
- Workstation assessment
- Eye tests if requested
- Regular breaks from screen
- Adjustable equipment

CCTV Operator Specific Hazards:
- Eye strain from monitors
- Repetitive strain injury
- Stress from viewing incidents
- Lone working (some control rooms)
- Sedentary work

Reporting Hazards:
- Report unsafe conditions
- Report equipment faults
- Use proper channels
- Don't ignore hazards
- Document concerns

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 1.2.5: REPORTING OF INJURIES, DISEASES AND DANGEROUS OCCURRENCES 
           REGULATIONS (RIDDOR) 2013
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

What is RIDDOR:
- Legal requirement to report certain incidents
- To Health and Safety Executive (HSE)
- Employer's responsibility
- Creates records for analysis

What Must Be Reported:

DEATHS:
- All work-related deaths
- Report immediately

SPECIFIED INJURIES:
- Fractures (except fingers, thumbs, toes)
- Amputations
- Serious burns
- Loss of consciousness
- Injury requiring hospitalisation 24+ hours

OVER 7 DAY INJURIES:
- Incapacitation for more than 7 consecutive days
- Report within 15 days

OCCUPATIONAL DISEASES:
- Certain listed diseases
- When linked to work activity

DANGEROUS OCCURRENCES:
- Near misses with potential for serious harm
- Equipment failures
- Specified list in regulations

CCTV Operator Context:
- Slip, trip, fall in control room
- Electrical incidents
- Fire incidents
- Violence at work
- Stress-related illness (some circumstances)

Reporting Process:
- Online reporting to HSE
- By telephone for serious incidents
- Keep records of report
- Employer's duty (not employee)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 1.2.6: MANAGEMENT OF HEALTH AND SAFETY AT WORK REGULATIONS 1999
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Key Requirements:

RISK ASSESSMENT:
- Identify hazards
- Assess risk to employees and others
- Record findings (5+ employees)
- Review regularly
- Implement controls

For CCTV Control Rooms:
- Workstation risks
- Display screen risks
- Lone working risks
- Violence and aggression
- Stress and wellbeing

Health Surveillance:
- Where risks identified
- Eye tests for DSE users
- Monitoring employee health

Competent Person:
- Employer must appoint
- To assist with health and safety
- May be internal or external
- Appropriate knowledge and skills

Information and Training:
- Employees must be informed of risks
- Training on safe procedures
- Updated when things change
- Induction for new starters

Emergency Procedures:
- Plans for emergencies
- Fire evacuation
- First aid arrangements
- Contact procedures

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 1.2.7: CORPORATE MANSLAUGHTER AND CORPORATE HOMICIDE ACT 2007
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

What is Corporate Manslaughter:
- Criminal offence for organisations
- When gross negligence causes death
- Breach of duty of care
- By senior management

Key Elements:
- Organisation owes duty of care
- Gross breach of duty
- Death results
- Senior management substantially responsible

Penalties:
- Unlimited fine
- Publicity order (must publicise conviction)
- Remedial order
- Reputational damage

Relevance to CCTV:
- Employer's duty to protect operators
- Safe working conditions
- Protection from violence
- Proper training and procedures
- Mental health and wellbeing

Personal Liability:
- Individuals can still be prosecuted
- Under Health and Safety at Work Act
- Gross negligence manslaughter
- Directors and senior managers

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 1.2.8: CRIMINAL LAW BASICS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Criminal vs Civil Law:

CRIMINAL LAW:
- State vs individual
- Prosecution by CPS
- Standard: Beyond reasonable doubt
- Penalties: Prison, fines, community orders
- Examples: Theft, assault, fraud

CIVIL LAW:
- Individual vs individual
- Claimant brings case
- Standard: Balance of probabilities
- Remedies: Compensation, injunctions
- Examples: Negligence, breach of contract

Burden of Proof:
- Criminal: Prosecution must prove guilt
- Beyond reasonable doubt (sure)
- Civil: Claimant must prove case
- Balance of probabilities (more likely than not)

Common Criminal Offences (awareness):
- Theft
- Assault
- Criminal damage
- Public order offences
- Drug offences
- Fraud

CCTV Operator Role:
- Observe and record
- Alert appropriate responders
- Preserve evidence
- Provide witness statements
- NOT to make arrests (usually)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 1.2.9: POWERS AND LIMITATIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

CCTV Operators Have NO Special Powers:
- Same powers as ordinary citizen
- Cannot detain people
- Cannot search people
- Cannot demand identification
- Cannot enter private property

What CCTV Operators CAN Do:
- Observe and monitor
- Record footage
- Alert police and responders
- Provide evidence
- Give witness statements
- Direct colleagues to incidents

Citizen's Arrest (awareness only):
- Section 24A PACE 1984
- Indictable offences only
- Must be necessary
- Rarely applicable to CCTV operators
- Usually direct police instead

Use of Reasonable Force:
- Self-defence only
- Must be proportionate
- Must be necessary
- CCTV operators rarely in this position
- Usually in control room, not on scene

Working Within Authority:
- Follow procedures
- Stay within role
- Don't exceed powers
- Call appropriate responders
- Document and evidence
