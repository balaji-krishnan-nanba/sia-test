WHY THIS CHAPTER MATTERS
------------------------
Resolving conflict effectively requires a problem-solving approach. Security 
officers who can find solutions that address underlying issues achieve 
better outcomes than those who simply try to "win" arguments. Problem-solving 
techniques help resolve situations with less confrontation, maintain 
relationships, and reduce the likelihood of future conflict.

TOPIC 3.3.1: THE WIN MODEL
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

What is the WIN Model?
- Structured approach to resolving conflict
- Focuses on understanding the situation
- Leads to finding solutions
- W-I-N stands for key steps

The WIN Model:

W - WHAT (What is their problem/concern?)
- Listen to understand
- What are they telling you?
- What do they want?
- What is the real issue?
- Look beyond the surface complaint

I - IMPACT (What is the impact on them?)
- How is it affecting them?
- Why does it matter to them?
- What are the consequences for them?
- Understand their perspective
- Show empathy

N - NEEDS (What do they need from you?)
- What outcome do they want?
- What can you do to help?
- What is within your power?
- What are the options?
- What would resolve this for them?

Using WIN:
- Listen first, don't jump to solutions
- Understand before being understood
- Shows respect and empathy
- Leads to appropriate solutions
- Works for most conflict situations

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 3.3.2: UNDERSTANDING THE PROBLEM
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Active Listening:
- Focus fully on the speaker
- Don't interrupt
- Show you're listening (nodding, eye contact)
- Reflect back what you've heard
- Ask clarifying questions

Asking Questions:
- Open questions (encourage talking)
- Closed questions (confirm facts)
- "What happened?"
- "How can I help?"
- "What would you like to happen?"
- Avoid "why" questions (can seem accusatory)

Identifying the Real Issue:
- Surface complaint may not be real issue
- Underlying concerns
- Emotional needs (respect, fairness)
- Practical needs (solution, compensation)
- Sometimes just want to be heard

Common Underlying Issues:
- Feeling disrespected
- Feeling ignored
- Perceived unfairness
- Loss of face
- Frustration at situation
- Fear or anxiety

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 3.3.3: FINDING SOLUTIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Problem-Solving Approach:
1. Understand the problem fully
2. Identify what they need
3. Consider what's possible
4. Offer options where you can
5. Agree on a way forward
6. Follow through

Types of Solutions:

GIVE THEM WHAT THEY WANT:
- If reasonable and possible
- Simplest resolution
- Check you have authority
- Document if needed

OFFER AN ALTERNATIVE:
- Can't do X, but can do Y
- Partial solution
- Compromise
- Different approach to same goal

EXPLAIN WHY NOT:
- If you can't help
- Genuine reason
- Empathetic delivery
- Offer what you CAN do

ESCALATE:
- Beyond your authority
- Complex issue
- Person requests manager
- Document and refer

DELAY:
- Need more information
- Need to check
- Cool-off period
- Set clear timescale

When There's No Solution:
- Be honest
- Empathise with frustration
- Explain constraints
- Don't make false promises
- Maintain professionalism

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 3.3.4: OFFERING CHOICES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Why Offer Choices:
- Gives person some control
- Reduces feeling of powerlessness
- More likely to cooperate
- Ownership of decision
- Avoids direct confrontation

Types of Choices:

POSITIVE CHOICES:
- "You can wait here or in the café"
- "Would you prefer to speak to the manager now or tomorrow?"
- Both options acceptable to you

LIMITED CHOICES:
- "You can leave voluntarily or I'll have to call the police"
- "You can lower your voice or we'll have to end this conversation"
- One option clearly better for them

FACE-SAVING CHOICES:
- Allow them to back down with dignity
- "Perhaps you'd like to step outside for some air?"
- "Would you like a moment to consider?"

Framing Choices:
- State positive option first
- Make preferred choice more attractive
- Be clear about consequences
- Follow through consistently

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 3.3.5: NEGOTIATION AND COMPROMISE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

When to Negotiate:
- Both parties have legitimate concerns
- There's room for flexibility
- Better than confrontation
- When you have authority to agree

Negotiation Principles:
- Focus on interests, not positions
- Look for mutual benefit
- Be willing to give something
- Keep communication open
- Stay calm and professional

What You Might Negotiate:
- Time (deadline, waiting period)
- Process (how something happens)
- Partial solutions
- Alternative approaches
- Future arrangements

What You Can't Negotiate:
- Law and regulations
- Safety requirements
- Core policies
- Criminal behaviour
- Things outside your authority

Compromise:
- Both sides give something
- Neither gets everything
- Acceptable to both
- Better than escalation
- Shows flexibility

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 3.3.6: SETTING BOUNDARIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Why Boundaries Matter:
- Clarity about what's acceptable
- Professional limits
- Legal limits
- Policy limits
- Personal limits

Setting Clear Boundaries:
- Be specific
- Be consistent
- Explain the boundary
- State consequences
- Follow through

Examples of Boundaries:
- "I can't allow you in without ID"
- "I'm happy to help, but I need you to lower your voice"
- "I won't tolerate threats"
- "That behaviour isn't acceptable here"

Delivering Boundaries:
- Firm but respectful
- Not aggressive
- Clear and unambiguous
- Give them chance to comply
- Don't back down inappropriately

When Boundaries Are Crossed:
- Remind of boundary
- State consequences
- Follow through
- Document
- Don't engage in argument about the boundary

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 3.3.7: FOLLOWING THROUGH
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Importance of Follow-Through:
- Maintains credibility
- Shows you meant it
- Prevents future problems
- Professional standard
- Fair and consistent

What to Follow Through On:
- Promises made
- Consequences stated
- Actions agreed
- Information to provide
- Referrals to others

If You Can't Follow Through:
- Explain why
- Apologise if appropriate
- Offer alternative
- Don't make promises you can't keep

Documentation:
- Record what was agreed
- Actions taken
- Outcomes
- Any ongoing issues

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 3.3.8: WHEN PROBLEM-SOLVING DOESN'T WORK
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Recognising Failure:
- Person not engaging
- Escalation despite efforts
- Threats continuing
- Violence imminent
- Repeated boundary violations

Alternative Approaches:
- Withdrawal (tactical retreat)
- Call for backup
- Call police
- Physical intervention (last resort)
- Remove yourself from situation

Knowing Your Limits:
- Can't resolve every situation
- Some people won't cooperate
- Some situations require police
- Safety comes first
- Don't feel you've failed

Learning from Outcomes:
- What worked?
- What didn't work?
- What could be done differently?
- Debrief and reflection
- Training and development
