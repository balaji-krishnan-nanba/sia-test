WHY THIS CHAPTER MATTERS
------------------------
Queue management is a fundamental skill for door supervisors. Poor queue 
management can lead to frustration, conflict, crushing injuries, blocking 
emergency routes, and complaints from neighbours. Good management ensures 
safety, fairness, and a positive customer experience.

TOPIC 2.10.1: PURPOSE OF QUEUE MANAGEMENT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Safety:
- Prevent crushing
- Prevent blocking roads/pavements
- Maintain clear emergency routes
- Control crowd density
- Prevent surges

Fairness:
- First come, first served principle
- Transparent system
- Consistent application
- Reduce frustration and conflict

Capacity Control:
- Manage entry rate
- Monitor venue capacity
- One-in-one-out when full
- Prevent overcrowding inside

Customer Experience:
- Reasonable wait times
- Information about wait
- Professional interaction
- Sets positive tone for night

Venue Reputation:
- Well-managed queue looks professional
- Reduces complaints
- Shows venue cares about customers
- Attracts quality customers

Legal Compliance:
- Capacity is legal limit
- Blocking highways is offence
- Noise disturbance to neighbours
- Fire exit access

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 2.10.2: CAPACITY LIMITS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

What Sets Capacity:
- Fire risk assessment
- Licensing conditions
- Venue's own policy (may be lower)
- Legal maximum cannot be exceeded

Why Capacity Matters:
- Fire safety - evacuation time
- Oxygen supply
- Crush prevention
- Customer comfort
- Legal requirement

Exceeding Capacity:
- Criminal offence
- Licence at risk
- Dangerous situation
- Door supervisor can face action

Monitoring Capacity:
- Clickers/counters
- Count people in
- Count people out
- Know current number
- Communicate with colleagues

One-In-One-Out:
- When at capacity
- Each person in = one person must leave
- Maintain exact count
- No exceptions

VIPs at Capacity:
- Capacity still applies
- Cannot exceed for anyone
- VIPs wait or operate one-in-one-out
- No exceptions regardless of status

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 2.10.3: QUEUE POSITIONING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Location Considerations:
- Away from roads/traffic
- Not blocking pavements
- Not blocking emergency exits
- Not blocking neighbouring businesses
- Consider local residents
- Visible from door

Barrier Systems:
- Retractable belt barriers
- Posts and ropes
- Metal barriers
- Create orderly queue shape
- Define clear path

Queue Route:
- Logical flow
- Single file or controlled width
- Leads directly to entry point
- No dead ends
- Easy for staff to monitor

Lighting:
- Good lighting for safety
- CCTV coverage
- Staff can see queue
- Customers feel safe
- Deters crime

Shelter:
- If possible, covered area
- Consideration in bad weather
- Speed up processing in rain
- Sympathetic to customers

Neighbouring Premises:
- Don't block their access
- Don't cause noise issues
- Be considerate
- Respond to complaints

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 2.10.4: COMMUNICATION WITH QUEUE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Keep Customers Informed:
- Approximate wait time
- Why there's a wait
- When capacity available
- Progress updates

Managing Expectations:
- Be honest about wait
- Don't promise what you can't deliver
- If unlikely to get in, say so
- Alternative venue suggestions

Professional Interaction:
- Polite and friendly
- Acknowledge customers
- Thank for patience
- Apologise for delays

Dealing with Frustration:
- Acknowledge frustration
- Explain reasons calmly
- Don't argue
- Remain professional

Communication Methods:
- Verbal announcements
- Speaking to individuals
- Radio to inside team
- Updates from colleagues

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 2.10.5: QUEUE JUMPING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Why It's a Problem:
- Unfair to others
- Causes resentment
- Can cause conflict
- Undermines system

Dealing with Queue Jumpers:
- Approach promptly
- Polite but firm
- "The queue starts at the back"
- Direct to correct position

If They Refuse:
- Remain calm
- Explain policy clearly
- May refuse entry
- Document if necessary
- Don't escalate unnecessarily

Preventing Queue Jumping:
- Clear barrier system
- Staff positioned along queue
- Visible monitoring
- Quick intervention

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 2.10.6: GUEST LISTS AND VIPs
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Guest List Entry:
- May have separate entrance
- Verify names on list
- Check ID matches name
- Still apply entry criteria

VIP Treatment:
- Faster entry (if not at capacity)
- May bypass main queue
- Still cannot exceed capacity
- Still apply rules (ID, sobriety)

Managing Main Queue Perception:
- Discrete VIP entry if possible
- Don't flaunt preferential treatment
- Acknowledge wait of main queue
- Fair treatment overall

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 2.10.7: REFUSING ENTRY IN QUEUE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

When to Refuse:
- Intoxication
- Already banned
- Dress code violation
- Aggressive behaviour
- Underage
- Capacity full

How to Refuse:
- Polite but clear
- Brief explanation if appropriate
- Don't argue or debate
- Firm and final
- Ask them to leave area

Documentation:
- Document significant refusals
- Especially if trouble anticipated
- Description of person
- Reason for refusal
- Their reaction

Watching After Refusal:
- Monitor they leave
- Watch for return to queue
- Watch for targeting of other customers
- Alert colleagues

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 2.10.8: WEATHER CONSIDERATIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Cold Weather:
- Speed up processing
- Allow waiting inside if possible
- Be sympathetic
- Health risks for prolonged wait

Hot Weather:
- Hydration available
- Shade if possible
- Watch for heat exhaustion
- Speed up entry

Rain:
- Shelter where possible
- Speed processing
- Sympathetic approach
- Consider health impacts

Impact on Behaviour:
- Weather affects mood
- Frustration increases
- Patience decreases
- Adjust approach accordingly

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 2.10.9: DISPERSAL
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

End of Night:
- Queue may form to leave
- Encourage people to move on
- Prevent congregation
- Arrange transport

Taxi Management:
- Designated taxi area
- Order of taxi queue
- Communication with taxi companies
- Safe waiting area

Noise Considerations:
- Neighbours affected
- Encourage quiet departure
- Address noisy customers
- Respect local area

Monitoring Outside:
- Watch for post-venue conflicts
- Prevent loitering
- Presence until crowd gone
- Report issues

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 2.10.10: PUBLIC ORDER OUTSIDE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Legal Position:
- Queue is usually public space
- Limited powers outside venue
- Cannot arrest for queue behaviour (unless indictable offence)
- Can refuse entry

Managing Disorder:
- De-escalation first
- Refuse entry if appropriate
- Call police for serious issues
- Do not chase people

Focus:
- Protecting venue and queue
- Don't pursue outside your area
- Gather information for police
- Be a good witness

Question 1 [Easy]
When a venue has reached its maximum capacity, what system should be 
operated for entry?

A) Allow VIPs in regardless of capacity
B) One-in-one-out
C) Close the venue to new entry
D) Increase the capacity limit temporarily

Correct Answer: B

Explanation: When at maximum capacity, a one-in-one-out system should be 
operated. This means for each person who enters, one person must have left. 
Capacity limits cannot be exceeded for anyone, including VIPs, and are set 
by fire risk assessment for safety reasons.
