WHY THIS CHAPTER MATTERS
------------------------
Door supervisors are uniquely positioned to identify and protect vulnerable 
people at licensed premises. This includes intoxicated persons, potential 
victims of sexual predators, victims of drink spiking, and those at risk 
of exploitation. Effective safeguarding can prevent serious harm.

TOPIC 2.9.1: UNDERSTANDING VULNERABILITY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Definition of Vulnerability:
- Temporary or permanent state
- Person unable to protect themselves from harm
- Unable to make sound decisions
- Unable to recognise danger
- At risk of exploitation

Vulnerability Can Be:
- Temporary (intoxication)
- Permanent (disability)
- Situational (separated from friends)
- Fluctuating (mental health)

Context of Licensed Premises:
- Late night environment
- Alcohol consumption
- Drug use (voluntary or involuntary)
- Crowded venues
- Predators target these environments

Door Supervisor Responsibility:
- Identify vulnerability
- Intervene appropriately
- Protect from harm
- Arrange safe outcomes
- Report concerns

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 2.9.2: TYPES OF VULNERABILITY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

INTOXICATION (Most Common at Venues):
- Alcohol impairment
- Drug impairment
- Combination of substances
- Impairs judgement and self-protection
- Risks: assault, robbery, sexual assault, injury, accident

AGE-RELATED:
- Under-18s (should not be in many venues)
- Young adults (less experienced)
- Elderly (confusion, falls, exploitation)

DISABILITY:
- Physical disabilities
- Learning disabilities
- Hearing/visual impairment
- Mental health conditions
- May be taken advantage of

MENTAL HEALTH:
- Mental health crisis
- Psychosis
- Suicidal thoughts
- Confusion
- Dementia

OTHER VULNERABILITY:
- Homeless individuals
- Non-English speakers
- Those under coercion/control
- Victims of trafficking
- Separated from group
- Lost or distressed

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 2.9.3: INDICATORS OF VULNERABILITY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Physical Signs:
- Extreme intoxication
- Loss of coordination
- Inability to stand/walk properly
- Slurred speech
- Confusion about location
- Cannot communicate clearly
- Vomiting
- Semi-conscious or unconscious

Behavioural Signs:
- Separated from friends
- Being "looked after" by strangers
- Distressed or crying
- Frightened or anxious
- Submissive to someone
- Not in control of situation
- Trying to leave with unknown person
- Signs of having been assaulted

Situational Signs:
- Alone late at night
- Lost or confused
- Phone/money/belongings missing
- Waiting for transport that hasn't arrived
- Being followed or watched
- Someone paying them excessive attention

Financial Signs:
- Someone else controlling their money
- Being bought excessive drinks
- No means to get home

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 2.9.4: DRINK SPIKING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Common Spiking Drugs:
- GHB/GBL (most common)
- Ketamine
- Rohypnol
- Extra alcohol in drinks
- Other sedatives

How Spiking Happens:
- Drug added to unattended drink
- Drug added while person distracted
- Stronger drink given than ordered
- Multiple shots added
- Predator often nearby watching

Signs of Drink Spiking:
- Sudden unexpected intoxication
- Intoxication inconsistent with amount consumed
- Sudden drowsiness
- Confusion/disorientation
- Loss of balance
- Nausea
- Memory gaps
- Feeling "strange" or "not right"
- Loss of inhibitions
- Unconsciousness

Response to Suspected Spiking:

1. TAKE PERSON TO SAFE AREA:
- Away from crowd
- With staff supervision

2. DO NOT LEAVE THEM ALONE:
- Stay with them or
- Ensure trusted person stays

3. DO NOT LET THEM LEAVE WITH STRANGER:
- Check identity of anyone trying to take them
- Verify relationship
- If in doubt, don't allow

4. CONSIDER AMBULANCE:
- If unresponsive
- If deteriorating
- If concerned about breathing
- GHB can cause unconsciousness quickly

5. PRESERVE THE DRINK:
- Don't throw it away
- Keep as evidence
- Give to police

6. REPORT:
- To management
- To police
- Document incident

Prevention:
- Watch for suspicious behaviour around drinks
- Note people watching others' drinks
- Notice if drinks left unattended
- Intervene if see something suspicious

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 2.9.5: SEXUAL PREDATORS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Predator Behaviour:
- Target intoxicated people
- Separate victim from friends
- Offer to "take care of" someone
- Buy excessive drinks
- Not drinking much themselves
- Overly attentive to drunk person
- Following someone who leaves alone
- Waiting for vulnerable person

Warning Signs:
- Person they're with seems too intoxicated
- Person seems uncomfortable
- Body language shows discomfort
- Unequal relationship apparent
- Victim seems confused about who person is
- Predator steering victim away from friends/exits

Door Supervisor Response:

1. INTERVENE:
- Approach and speak to potential victim
- "Are you okay?"
- "Do you know this person?"
- "Can I help you find your friends?"

2. SEPARATE IF NEEDED:
- Ask to speak to potential victim alone
- Away from suspected predator
- Check their wellbeing privately

3. VERIFY RELATIONSHIP:
- "How do you know this person?"
- "Where did you meet?"
- "Are you comfortable with them?"

4. PREVENT DEPARTURE WITH PREDATOR:
- If victim is too intoxicated to consent to leaving
- If they seem unsure/uncomfortable
- Arrange alternative safe transport
- Contact friends/family

5. ALERT COLLEAGUES:
- Point out suspected predator
- Monitor their behaviour
- Consider ejection if persistent

6. REPORT:
- To management
- To police if concerned
- Document incident

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 2.9.6: CHILD SEXUAL EXPLOITATION (CSE)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Definition:
- Under-18s manipulated into sexual activity
- Often involves exchange (money, gifts, affection)
- Victim may not recognise they're being exploited
- Grooming process involved

Signs of CSE (may occur around venues):
- Young person with older "boyfriend/girlfriend"
- Under-18s being bought alcohol
- Young person controlled by adult
- Signs of physical abuse
- Multiple phones
- Going missing frequently
- Secretive about relationships
- Older adults hanging around young people
- Unexplained gifts or money

Door Supervisor Action:
- Be vigilant for signs
- Report concerns to management
- Report to police
- Don't ignore warning signs
- Under-18s shouldn't be in adult venues

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 2.9.7: COUNTY LINES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Definition:
- Drug dealing model
- Uses dedicated phone lines
- Transports drugs from cities to smaller areas
- Exploits children and vulnerable adults
- Often involves violence and coercion

Signs:
- Young person dealing drugs
- Signs of violence or abuse
- Appearing scared or controlled
- New to area, no connections
- Multiple phones
- Unexplained injuries
- Withdrawn behaviour

Response:
- Report to police (not just eject)
- These are victims as well as offenders
- Safeguarding concern
- Document observations

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 2.9.8: MODERN SLAVERY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Forms:
- Human trafficking
- Forced labour
- Sexual exploitation
- Domestic servitude
- Criminal exploitation

Indicators:
- Signs of physical abuse
- Malnourished appearance
- Withdrawn, frightened
- Controlled by another person
- Unable to speak for themselves
- Someone else holds their ID/documents
- Inappropriate clothing for conditions
- Living and working at same address
- Doesn't know their address
- Limited English, fearful

Response:
- Report concerns to police
- Modern Slavery Helpline: 08000 121 700
- Do not confront suspected traffickers
- Subtle intervention with victim if safe
- Document observations

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 2.9.9: DOOR SUPERVISOR SAFEGUARDING DUTIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Be Vigilant:
- Constantly observe for vulnerability
- Watch for predatory behaviour
- Notice changes in behaviour
- Trust your instincts

Intervene:
- Check on vulnerable people
- Ask if they're okay
- Offer assistance
- Separate from concerning person

Prevent Harm:
- Don't let vulnerable person leave with stranger
- Arrange safe transport
- Contact friends/family
- Keep them safe until sorted

Arrange Safe Outcome:
- Taxi to known address
- Friend/family to collect
- Stay until safely departed
- Waiting area inside if needed

Report:
- To management
- To police for serious concerns
- Document all incidents
- Share intelligence

Trust Your Instincts:
- If something seems wrong, investigate
- Better to check and be wrong
- Don't dismiss concerns
- Act early

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 2.9.10: SAFE DISPERSAL
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

End of Night Risks:
- Intoxicated people leaving
- Vulnerable people alone
- Predators waiting outside
- Cold weather exposure
- Transport difficulties

Safe Dispersal Measures:
- Taxi ranks / phone numbers
- Lighting around venue
- Staff presence outside
- Waiting area inside
- Monitoring departure areas
- Communication with taxi companies

Vulnerable Person Leaving:
- Are they able to get home safely?
- Do they have transport arranged?
- Is someone safe collecting them?
- Should we call taxi for them?
- Do they need to wait inside?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 2.9.11: ASK FOR ANGELA
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

What is "Ask for Angela":
- Code phrase for seeking help
- Used when someone feels unsafe
- Ask staff for "Angela"
- Staff then discreetly help

Staff Response:
- Recognise the code phrase
- Take person to safe area
- Ask how you can help
- May call taxi
- May separate from concerning person
- May call police if needed
- Discreet - don't make scene

Training:
- All staff should know scheme
- How to respond
- Discreet assistance
- Confidential

A customer approaches a staff member and asks for "Angela." What does

discreetly helping the person, which may include calling them a taxi or
