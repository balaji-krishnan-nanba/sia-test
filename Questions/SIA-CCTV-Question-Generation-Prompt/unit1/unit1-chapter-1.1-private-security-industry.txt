TOPIC 1.1.1: THE SECURITY INDUSTRY AUTHORITY (SIA)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

What is the SIA:
- Non-departmental public body
- Reports to the Home Secretary
- Established under Private Security Industry Act 2001
- Headquarters in London
- Operational since 2003

SIA's Main Responsibilities:
- Licensing individuals in the private security industry
- Managing the Approved Contractor Scheme (ACS)
- Setting and maintaining standards
- Working with police and partners
- Compliance and enforcement

SIA's Purpose:
- Protect the public
- Raise standards in private security
- Reduce criminality in the industry
- Ensure proper vetting of operatives
- Promote best practice

Who the SIA Regulates:
- Door supervisors
- Security guards
- CCTV operators (public space surveillance)
- Close protection operatives
- Cash and valuables in transit
- Key holders (in some circumstances)

Why CCTV Operators Need Licensing:
- Access to sensitive surveillance footage
- Power to direct police and responders
- Privacy implications
- Public trust in CCTV systems
- Potential for misuse of footage

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 1.1.2: THE PRIVATE SECURITY INDUSTRY ACT 2001
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Purpose of the Act:
- Created the SIA
- Established licensing requirements
- Set out offences and penalties
- Framework for regulation
- Defines "licensable activities"

Licensable Activities:
- Manned guarding (security guards)
- Door supervision
- Close protection
- Cash and valuables in transit
- Public space surveillance (CCTV)
- Immobilisation of vehicles

Public Space Surveillance - Definition:
- Guarding premises by CCTV
- Monitoring activities in public spaces
- Identifying persons from CCTV images
- Control room based operations
- Not private/domestic CCTV

What is NOT Licensable:
- In-house CCTV monitoring (employees only)
- Private domestic CCTV
- Retail CCTV (own premises only)
- Traffic monitoring (highways)
- CCTV for own business security

Key Offences Under the Act:

WORKING WITHOUT A LICENCE:
- Criminal offence
- Up to 5 years imprisonment
- Unlimited fine
- Applies to individual operative

EMPLOYING UNLICENSED STAFF:
- Criminal offence for employer
- Up to 5 years imprisonment
- Unlimited fine
- Strict liability offence

USING FALSE LICENCE:
- Serious criminal offence
- Up to 10 years imprisonment
- Fraud charges may apply

Enforcement:
- SIA compliance officers
- Police partnerships
- Unannounced inspections
- Intelligence-led operations
- Prosecution or civil sanctions

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 1.1.3: SIA LICENSING REQUIREMENTS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

To Obtain a CCTV Licence:

IDENTITY CHECK:
- Proof of identity
- Right to work in UK
- Passport, driving licence, etc.
- Biometric checks

CRIMINAL RECORD CHECK:
- Enhanced DBS (Disclosure and Barring Service)
- Spent and unspent convictions considered
- Barred list check
- Police intelligence

TRAINING REQUIREMENT:
- Level 2 Award for CCTV Operators
- Approved training provider
- Written examination
- Must pass before applying

APPLICATION PROCESS:
- Online application to SIA
- Fee payment
- Document submission
- Processing time varies

Licence Duration:
- Valid for 3 years
- Must renew before expiry
- New checks at renewal
- Cannot work if expired

Licence Conditions:
- Must carry licence when working
- Display badge when required
- Produce for inspection on request
- Report changes (address, name, convictions)

Refusing/Revoking Licences:
- Criminal convictions (especially violence, dishonesty, drugs)
- Connections to organised crime
- Mental health concerns affecting fitness
- Failing to meet ongoing conditions
- False information on application

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 1.1.4: THE SIA LICENCE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Physical Licence:
- Credit card sized
- Photograph of holder
- Unique licence number
- Expiry date
- Licence type (CCTV)
- SIA hologram (security feature)

Licence Display:
- Must be visible when working
- Worn on upper body typically
- Badge holder/armband
- Must not be obscured
- Producing on request

Licence Verification:
- Public can check licence online
- SIA licence checker website
- Enter licence number
- Confirms validity and type
- Employers should verify

What Licence Shows:
- Photo matches holder
- Type of work authorised (CCTV)
- Licence is current (not expired)
- Issued by genuine SIA

Lost/Stolen Licence:
- Report to SIA immediately
- Can apply for replacement
- Fee for replacement
- Keep working (with proof of application)

Changes to Report:
- Change of address
- Change of name
- New criminal convictions
- Contact details
- Failure to report is offence

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 1.1.5: APPROVED CONTRACTOR SCHEME (ACS)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

What is ACS:
- Voluntary scheme for security companies
- Shows commitment to quality
- Higher standards than minimum
- Assessed by UKAS-accredited bodies

Benefits of ACS:
- Recognised quality mark
- Competitive advantage
- Demonstrates professionalism
- Buyer confidence
- May be required for contracts

ACS Requirements:
- All staff properly licensed
- Quality management systems
- Health and safety standards
- Training and development
- Business integrity

Why Work for ACS Company:
- Higher standards
- Better training typically
- Professional environment
- Career development
- Industry recognition

ACS and CCTV:
- Control room companies can be ACS
- Local authority CCTV may require ACS
- Shows compliance standards
- Regular audits maintain status

TOPIC 1.1.6: THE PRIVATE SECURITY INDUSTRY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Sectors of Private Security:
- Manned guarding
- Door supervision
- CCTV/surveillance
- Close protection
- Cash handling
- Event security
- Specialist services

CCTV Sector Specifically:
- Town centre schemes
- Local authority control rooms
- Transport hubs (stations, airports)
- Shopping centres
- Corporate control rooms
- Event monitoring

Growth of CCTV Industry:
- UK has extensive CCTV coverage
- Estimated 4-6 million cameras
- Increasing demand for operators
- Technology advancing rapidly
- Integration with other systems

Career in CCTV:
- Entry level: CCTV operator
- Senior operator
- Shift supervisor
- Control room manager
- Regional manager
- Specialist roles (analyst, investigator)

Professional Bodies:
- Security Institute
- ASIS International
- British Security Industry Association (BSIA)
- Continuing professional development

Industry Standards:
- BS 7958 (CCTV management and operation)
- BS 7499 (Static site guarding)
- ISO quality standards
- Surveillance Camera Code of Practice

TOPIC 1.1.7: REGULATION AND PUBLIC TRUST
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Why Regulation Matters for CCTV:
- Privacy implications significant
- Access to personal data (footage)
- Power to direct police action
- Can affect people's liberty
- Must balance security and rights

Public Confidence:
- People expect professional operators
- Trust in CCTV systems
- Appropriate use of surveillance
- Data protection compliance
- Proportionate monitoring

Professionalism in CCTV:
- Licensed and vetted staff
- Proper training
- Following procedures
- Respecting privacy
- Confidentiality

Consequences of Poor Practice:
- Loss of public trust
- Legal action
- Criminal prosecution
- Industry reputation damage
- Individual licence revocation

Partnership Working:
- Police integration
- Local authority links
- Retail radio schemes
- Emergency services
- Community safety teams
