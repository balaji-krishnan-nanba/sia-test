KEY PRINCIPLE
-------------
Disengagement (breakaway) techniques are about ESCAPING from situations 
where you are grabbed, held, or attacked. The priority is creating 
distance and getting to safety. These are defensive, not offensive 
techniques.

TOPIC 4.3.1: PURPOSE OF DISENGAGEMENT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

What is Disengagement:
- Breaking free from grabs or holds
- Creating distance from attacker
- Escaping dangerous situation
- Regaining control of your space

Goals of Disengagement:
- Get free from grip
- Create reactionary gap
- Escape to safety
- Get help
- Reassess situation

When to Use Disengagement:
- Someone grabs you
- You're being held
- You're in danger
- Need to escape
- Attack in progress

Disengagement is NOT:
- Offensive attack
- Punishment
- Restraint technique
- Aggressive response
- The end of the interaction (just creates space)

After Disengagement - Options:
- Escape and get help
- Create distance and reassess
- Call for backup
- Verbal de-escalation from safe distance
- Further physical intervention if necessary

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 4.3.2: PRINCIPLES OF BREAKAWAY TECHNIQUES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Key Principles:

ESCAPE AT WEAKEST POINT:
- Every grip has a weak point
- Usually where fingers meet thumb
- Apply force at this point
- Much easier than fighting strength

SPEED AND DECISIVENESS:
- Act quickly
- Commit fully to technique
- Hesitation reduces effectiveness
- Explosive action

USE OF WHOLE BODY:
- Not just arms
- Core strength
- Body weight
- Direction of travel
- Rotation adds power

ELEMENT OF SURPRISE:
- Attacker not expecting resistance
- Their grip is passive
- You have advantage
- Act before they tighten grip

FOLLOW THROUGH:
- Complete the technique
- Don't stop halfway
- Create sufficient distance
- Move away immediately

The Weak Point Rule:
- Thumb is weakest part of grip
- Fingers can't oppose thumb effectively
- Pull toward gap between thumb and fingers
- Twist/rotate through this point

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 4.3.3: COMMON GRABS AND RESPONSES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

WRIST GRAB (Single Hand):
- Attacker holds your wrist with one hand
- Weak point: thumb side
- Rotate wrist toward their thumb
- Pull sharply through gap
- Step away creating distance

WRIST GRAB (Both Hands):
- Attacker holds one wrist with both hands
- Stronger grip
- Same principle - through the thumbs
- Bring second hand over to assist
- Use body weight to pull through

CLOTHING GRAB (Single Lapel):
- Attacker grabs clothing at chest
- Control their gripping hand
- Step to side
- Release grip and create distance
- Watch for secondary attack

CLOTHING GRAB (Double Lapels):
- Both lapels grabbed
- More aggressive intent
- Control their hands/wrists
- Step and rotate
- Break grip and distance

HAIR GRAB:
- Extremely painful
- First action: control their hand
- Press their hand to your head (reduces pain)
- Turn into them
- Create leverage to escape

BEAR HUG (Arms Free):
- Grabbed from front or behind
- Arms not pinned
- Lower centre of gravity (squat)
- Push hands/arms away
- Step out and create distance

BEAR HUG (Arms Pinned):
- More dangerous position
- Drop weight immediately
- Make yourself heavy
- Move hips to create space
- Work arms free
- Watch for lift

CHOKE (Front):
- Life-threatening
- Immediate response required
- Chin down, shoulders up
- Create space at neck
- Break grip and escape

CHOKE (Rear):
- Life-threatening
- Protect airway
- Turn into them if possible
- Break grip urgently
- This is emergency

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 4.3.4: PROTECTING VITAL AREAS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

What to Protect:
- Head/face
- Neck/throat
- Spine
- Groin
- Joints

During Disengagement:
- Keep chin down (protects throat)
- Shoulders up (protects neck)
- Cover face if strikes coming
- Protect spine from impact
- Don't hyperextend joints

If Strikes Coming:
- Cover and move
- Forearms protecting face
- Elbows protecting body
- Keep moving
- Don't just stand and take hits

Creating Barriers:
- Arms as barrier
- Keep object between you and attacker
- Use environment
- Table, chair, barrier
- Gives you time

Head Protection Priority:
- Head injuries can be fatal
- Concussion affects function
- Can't fight effectively if head injured
- Protect at all costs

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 4.3.5: CREATING DISTANCE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Reactionary Gap:
- After breaking free, create space
- At least arm's length
- Ideally more
- Gives time to react

Explosive Movement:
- Don't slowly back away
- Explosive move to distance
- Change angle (don't just go straight back)
- Makes following harder

Direction of Travel:
- Generally away from attacker
- Toward help/colleagues
- Toward exit if escaping
- Not into corner
- Not into hazards

Maintaining Awareness:
- Keep attacker in view
- Don't turn your back (initially)
- Peripheral awareness of surroundings
- Ready for follow-up attack

Distance is Safety:
- Most attacks are close range
- Distance defeats most attacks
- Gives you options
- Time to think and react

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 4.3.6: GROUND DEFENCE BASICS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Avoid Going to Ground:
- Ground is dangerous
- Multiple attackers = very dangerous
- Hard to escape
- Hard to see surroundings
- Limited movement

If Taken to Ground:
- Protect head immediately
- Cover up
- Create barrier with limbs
- Try to get up ASAP
- Shout for help

Getting Back Up:
- Don't stay down
- Create space first
- Use one arm as barrier
- Push up and move away
- Keep facing threat

Ground Hazards:
- Kicks while down
- Stomping
- Multiple attackers
- Can't use environment
- Hard to break fall

Against Wall:
- Can be trapped
- But wall protects back
- Can push off wall
- Slide along to escape
- Don't let them pin you

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 4.3.7: ENVIRONMENTAL CONSIDERATIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Use the Environment:
- Put objects between you
- Tables, chairs as barriers
- Doors (escape through/close)
- Corners (can work against you)
- Lighting (they can't hit what they can't see)

Hazards to Avoid:
- Wet floors (slipping)
- Glass (bottles, glasses, windows)
- Stairs (falling)
- Sharp edges
- Crowded areas

Escape Routes:
- Know exits
- Clear paths
- Don't get cornered
- Move toward help

Bystanders:
- May help you
- May help attacker
- May become targets
- Be aware of their position
- Don't put them at risk

Weapons in Environment:
- Bottles and glasses (common)
- Furniture
- Pool cues
- Keys
- Be aware of what could be used

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 4.3.8: DEALING WITH MULTIPLE ATTACKERS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Multiple Attackers - Most Dangerous:
- Overwhelmed quickly
- Can't watch all
- Harder to defend
- Must not go to ground

Principles:

ESCAPE IS PRIORITY:
- Don't try to fight all of them
- Break free and run
- Get to safety
- Get help

POSITIONING:
- Try to keep them in front
- Line them up (one behind other)
- Don't let them surround
- Back to wall if trapped

MOVEMENT:
- Keep moving
- Harder to coordinate against
- Don't stand still
- Move around obstacles

TARGET SELECTION:
- If must engage, focus on one
- Preferably the leader
- Or the closest
- Don't split attention

SHOUTING:
- Attract attention
- May deter attackers
- May bring help
- Shows you're resisting

When Outnumbered:
- Accept you can't win fighting
- Focus on escape
- Tactical withdrawal
- Live to work another day

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 4.3.9: FOLLOW-UP ACTIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

After Breaking Free:

IMMEDIATE:
- Create distance
- Assess threat
- Assess yourself (injuries?)
- Get to safe position
- Call for backup

CONTINUE OR DISENGAGE:
- Is threat neutralised?
- Can you safely re-engage?
- Should you wait for help?
- Is escape the best option?

VERBAL COMMANDS:
- "STAY BACK!"
- "DON'T COME CLOSER!"
- Warning before any further action
- Witnesses hear you

RE-ENGAGEMENT:
- Only if necessary
- Only if safe
- Only if legally justified
- May need different approach

ESCAPE ENTIRELY:
- Sometimes best option
- Not weakness
- Professional decision
- Get help and return with backup

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 4.3.10: TRAINING AND PREPARATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Why Training Matters:
- Techniques must be practiced
- Stress affects performance
- Muscle memory required
- Builds confidence
- Identifies weaknesses

Regular Practice:
- Refresher training
- Practice with resistance
- Different scenarios
- Build automatic responses

Stress Inoculation:
- Training under pressure
- Builds coping mechanisms
- Reduces freeze response
- More realistic preparation

Physical Fitness:
- Improves effectiveness
- More strength and endurance
- Better balance
- Faster reaction time

Mental Preparation:
- Visualisation
- Pre-planning responses
- Acceptance that violence may occur
- Confidence in abilities

Limitations:
- Know what you can't do
- Size/strength limits
- Skill limits
- When to get help
- When to retreat
