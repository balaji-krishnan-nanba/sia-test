WHY THIS CHAPTER MATTERS
------------------------
Close Protection Operatives must understand the legal framework within which
they operate. The law defines their powers and limitations, protects their
actions when using force, and places obligations regarding data protection,
human rights, and equality. Legal knowledge protects both the CPO and the
principal.

TOPIC 1.2.1: PRIVATE SECURITY INDUSTRY ACT 2001 (PSIA)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Purpose of the Act:
- Regulate private security industry
- Establish the Security Industry Authority
- Define licensable activities
- Create offences for non-compliance
- Raise standards and professionalism

The Security Industry Authority:
- Created by PSIA 2001
- Regulatory body
- Issues and revokes licences
- Sets training standards
- Conducts compliance checks
- Reports to Home Office

Licensable Activities for CP:
- Guarding individuals against assault
- Guarding individuals against injury
- Under contract for services
- Protection of one or more individuals

Offences Under PSIA 2001:

WORKING WITHOUT A LICENCE:
- Criminal offence
- Up to 5 years imprisonment
- Unlimited fine
- Both apply

EMPLOYING UNLICENSED OPERATIVES:
- Criminal offence
- Up to 5 years imprisonment
- Unlimited fine
- Employer liability

USING FALSE/ALTERED LICENCE:
- Serious criminal offence
- Up to 10 years imprisonment
- Fraud implications

Licence Conditions:
- Must display licence when working
- Notify SIA of changes (address, convictions)
- Valid for 3 years
- Renewal required before expiry
- Can be revoked for misconduct

Approved Contractor Scheme (ACS):
- Voluntary scheme
- Companies meeting high standards
- Regular assessment
- Quality mark
- Often preferred by clients

TOPIC 1.2.2: HUMAN RIGHTS ACT 1998
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Introduction:
- Incorporates European Convention on Human Rights
- All public bodies must comply
- Private security has indirect obligations
- Affects how CPOs operate

Key Articles for Close Protection:

ARTICLE 2 - RIGHT TO LIFE:
- Most fundamental right
- Duty to protect life
- Justifies protective measures
- Can use force to protect life
- CPO's PRIMARY purpose
- Must take reasonable steps

ARTICLE 3 - PROHIBITION OF TORTURE:
- No torture or inhuman treatment
- No degrading treatment
- Absolute right
- Cannot be breached in any circumstances
- Affects how force is used

ARTICLE 5 - RIGHT TO LIBERTY:
- No arbitrary detention
- Limits on holding people
- Arrest must be lawful
- CPOs cannot detain without cause
- Citizen's arrest limitations

ARTICLE 6 - RIGHT TO A FAIR TRIAL:
- Fair hearing
- Evidence must be lawful
- Affects evidence gathering
- Note-taking and statements

ARTICLE 8 - RIGHT TO PRIVATE LIFE:
- Privacy in private life
- Family life protected
- Home protected
- Correspondence protected
- Surveillance implications
- Must be proportionate

ARTICLE 10 - FREEDOM OF EXPRESSION:
- Free speech
- Media rights
- Cannot prevent lawful reporting
- Public figures have reduced privacy

ARTICLE 14 - NON-DISCRIMINATION:
- Rights apply equally
- No discrimination
- Protected characteristics
- Fair treatment for all

Balancing Rights:
- Rights can conflict
- Principal's safety vs others' rights
- Proportionality test
- Necessary in democratic society
- Legitimate aim required

TOPIC 1.2.3: CRIMINAL LAW - USE OF FORCE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Common Law Self-Defence:
- Ancient legal principle
- Defence of self
- Defence of another
- Can use reasonable force
- Must honestly believe necessary
- Subjective test (what you believed)

Criminal Law Act 1967 - Section 3:
- Prevention of crime
- Lawful arrest
- "Such force as is reasonable"
- Objective test for reasonableness
- Key statute for CPOs

Elements of Lawful Force:

NECESSITY:
- Force must be necessary
- No reasonable alternative
- Cannot use if can withdraw
- Last resort principle

PROPORTIONALITY:
- Force must be proportionate
- Match force to threat
- Cannot use excessive force
- Minimum force principle

HONEST BELIEF:
- Must genuinely believe
- Threat was real
- Force was necessary
- Mistake of fact may be defence

PRE-EMPTIVE ACTION:
- Do not have to wait to be hit
- Can strike first if attack imminent
- Must genuinely believe attack coming
- Still must be proportionate

What Force Can Be Used For:
- Self-defence
- Defence of another (including principal)
- Defence of property (limited)
- Prevention of crime
- Effecting lawful arrest
- Preventing escape from arrest

Excessive Force:
- Using more than necessary
- Disproportionate to threat
- Could face criminal charges
- Could face civil claims
- Assault, ABH, GBH possible

TOPIC 1.2.4: CRIMINAL LAW - CITIZEN'S ARREST
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Power of Arrest - Section 24A PACE 1984:
- Any person can arrest
- Limited circumstances
- Higher threshold than police

Conditions for Citizen's Arrest:

1. INDICTABLE OFFENCE:
   - Must be indictable offence
   - Committed or being committed
   - Serious offences only
   - Summary only offences NOT covered

2. REASONABLE GROUNDS:
   - Believe person guilty
   - Reasonably believe
   - Not just suspicion

3. NECESSITY:
   - Impracticable for constable to arrest
   - Prevent: physical injury, loss/damage
   - Prevent: escape before constable arrives
   - Police not immediately available

4. USE OF FORCE:
   - Only reasonable force
   - Proportionate
   - Minimum necessary

Risks of Citizen's Arrest:
- False imprisonment claims
- Assault claims if excessive force
- Compensation claims
- Criminal prosecution
- Professional embarrassment
- SIA licence implications

When to Arrest:
- Serious offences only
- Clear evidence
- Threat to principal
- Threat to others
- Police not available
- As last resort

When NOT to Arrest:
- Minor offences
- Insufficient evidence
- Police available
- Risk too high
- Better alternatives exist

Procedure After Arrest:
- Contact police immediately
- Explain reason for arrest
- Use minimum restraint
- Monitor welfare
- Handover to police ASAP
- Make notes contemporaneously

TOPIC 1.2.5: CRIMINAL LAW - RELEVANT OFFENCES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Offences Against the Person:

COMMON ASSAULT:
- Any unlawful force
- Or threat causing fear
- Summary offence
- Magistrates' Court

ASSAULT OCCASIONING ABH:
- Actual bodily harm
- More than trivial injury
- Either-way offence
- Up to 5 years imprisonment

GRIEVOUS BODILY HARM (S.20):
- Serious injury
- Wounding
- Either-way offence
- Up to 5 years imprisonment

GBH WITH INTENT (S.18):
- Intent to cause GBH
- Most serious assault
- Indictable only
- Up to life imprisonment

AFFRAY:
- Unlawful violence
- Causing fear
- Public disorder element
- Either-way offence

Other Relevant Offences:

HARASSMENT:
- Protection from Harassment Act 1997
- Course of conduct
- Causes alarm or distress
- Stalking provisions
- Restraining orders

CRIMINAL DAMAGE:
- Damaging property
- Without lawful excuse
- Defence of property possible

PUBLIC ORDER OFFENCES:
- Threatening behaviour
- Disorderly conduct
- Fear or provocation of violence

OFFENSIVE WEAPONS:
- Prevention of Crime Act 1953
- No offensive weapon in public
- Without lawful authority
- Without reasonable excuse

TOPIC 1.2.6: CIVIL LAW
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Criminal vs Civil Law:

CRIMINAL LAW:
- State vs individual
- Beyond reasonable doubt
- Prosecution
- Punishment (prison, fine)
- Police investigate

CIVIL LAW:
- Individual vs individual
- Balance of probabilities
- Claimant vs defendant
- Compensation (damages)
- Private action

Civil Claims Against CPOs:

NEGLIGENCE:
- Duty of care
- Breach of duty
- Damage caused
- Foreseeable harm
- Compensation claims

TRESPASS TO THE PERSON:
- Assault (threat)
- Battery (contact)
- False imprisonment
- No consent

FALSE IMPRISONMENT:
- Unlawful detention
- No consent
- No lawful authority
- Can arise from citizen's arrest

TRESPASS TO LAND:
- Entering without permission
- Remaining after asked to leave
- Limiting factor for CPOs

Defences in Civil Law:
- Self-defence
- Defence of another
- Consent
- Lawful authority
- Necessity

Vicarious Liability:
- Employer liable for employee actions
- In course of employment
- Insurance important
- Why companies need ACS

TOPIC 1.2.7: DATA PROTECTION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

UK General Data Protection Regulation (UK GDPR):
- Regulates personal data
- Information Commissioner oversight
- Heavy penalties for breach
- Applies to CPOs

What is Personal Data:
- Information identifying individual
- Names, addresses, photos
- Vehicle registrations
- Surveillance footage
- Movements and locations

Data Protection Principles:

1. LAWFULNESS, FAIRNESS, TRANSPARENCY
2. PURPOSE LIMITATION
3. DATA MINIMISATION
4. ACCURACY
5. STORAGE LIMITATION
6. SECURITY
7. ACCOUNTABILITY

CPO Obligations:
- Only collect necessary data
- Keep information secure
- Don't share without authority
- Dispose of securely
- Report breaches

Lawful Basis:
- Consent
- Contract
- Legal obligation
- Vital interests (protecting life)
- Public task
- Legitimate interests

Surveillance and Data Protection:
- Recording is data processing
- Must have lawful basis
- Retention periods apply
- Subject access rights
- Must be proportionate

TOPIC 1.2.8: EQUALITY ACT 2010
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Purpose:
- Prevents discrimination
- Protects individuals
- Applies to service provision
- Applies to employment

Protected Characteristics:
1. Age
2. Disability
3. Gender reassignment
4. Marriage and civil partnership
5. Pregnancy and maternity
6. Race
7. Religion or belief
8. Sex
9. Sexual orientation

Types of Discrimination:

DIRECT:
- Treating someone less favourably
- Because of protected characteristic
- Compared to others

INDIRECT:
- Policy applying to all
- Disadvantages protected group
- Cannot be justified

HARASSMENT:
- Unwanted conduct
- Related to protected characteristic
- Violates dignity
- Creates hostile environment

VICTIMISATION:
- Treating someone badly
- Because they made complaint
- Or supported complaint

CPO Application:
- Treat all fairly
- No profiling based on characteristics
- Provide equal protection
- Challenge discrimination
- Report concerns

Reasonable Adjustments:
- For disabled persons
- Remove disadvantages
- Provide aids
- Adjust practices

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC 1.2.9: REGULATION OF INVESTIGATORY POWERS ACT 2000 (RIPA)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

What RIPA Covers:
- Surveillance activities
- Interception of communications
- Authorisation requirements
- Primarily law enforcement

When RIPA Applies:
- Covert surveillance
- Directed surveillance
- Intrusive surveillance
- Interception of communications

CPO Considerations:
- Overt protection not covered
- Covert surveillance needs authority
- Usually police/agency matter
- Don't conduct unauthorised surveillance

Surveillance Types:

DIRECTED SURVEILLANCE:
- Covert observation
- Likely to obtain private information
- For specific investigation
- Pre-planned

INTRUSIVE SURVEILLANCE:
- In residential premises
- In private vehicles
- More strictly regulated
- Usually police only

Lawful Surveillance by CPOs:
- Overt observation (normal duties)
- Counter-surveillance (detecting others)
- With proper authorisation
- Within employment scope
